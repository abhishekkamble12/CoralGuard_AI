## Standard Operating Procedure: Coral Bleaching Response

### 1. Detection & Assessment
When coral bleaching is detected through visual or automated monitoring:
1. Record the GPS coordinates, depth, and extent of bleaching observed.
2. Capture high-resolution images from multiple angles for AI classification.
3. Measure in-situ water temperature, salinity, and turbidity if instruments are available.
4. Note the percentage of colony affected: <25% (mild), 25-50% (moderate), >50% (severe).

### 2. Risk Level Classification
- **Low Risk (SSTA < 0.5°C)**: No active bleaching. Reef appears healthy with normal coloration. Continue routine monitoring at standard intervals (weekly).
- **Elevated Risk (SSTA 0.5–1.0°C)**: Early signs of paling or stress coloration visible. Increase monitoring to every 48 hours. Notify regional marine park rangers. Document progression with time-stamped photography.
- **Critical Risk (SSTA > 1.0°C, TSA > 8)**: Active mass bleaching event. Immediate notification to marine authority required within 2 hours. Deploy rapid-response field teams. Consider temporary closure of affected reef zones to tourism and fishing.

### 3. Immediate Response Actions
For Elevated Risk:
- Increase monitoring frequency to every 48 hours.
- Deploy additional temperature loggers in the affected area.
- Notify local dive operators and fishing communities.
- Begin documenting species-specific responses for scientific record.

For Critical Risk:
- Activate the Emergency Response Protocol (see emergency_response.txt).
- Contact the Marine Park Authority immediately (see authority_contacts.txt).
- Request emergency closure of affected zones.
- Mobilize coral fragment rescue teams to salvage genetic material from severely affected colonies.
- Establish a media blackout until official statements are prepared by the authority.

### 4. Post-Event Monitoring
After the thermal stress event subsides:
- Continue monitoring for 90 days to assess recovery trajectory.
- Document mortality rates by species.
- Map surviving colonies as potential climate-resilient genotypes.
- Submit a formal incident report to the national marine science agency within 30 days.

### 5. Documentation Requirements
All bleaching events must be documented with:
- Date, time, GPS coordinates, and depth.
- AI classification output (Healthy/Bleached/Dead with confidence score).
- Environmental parameters (SSTA, TSA, salinity, turbidity, pH).
- Photographs (minimum 4 per affected area).
- Observer name and affiliation.
